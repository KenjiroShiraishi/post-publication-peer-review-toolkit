# Post-Publication Peer Review Toolkit

This repository provides practical tools for readers, clinicians, students, and early-career researchers who wish to evaluate published academic articles after publication.

The focus is not on personal accusation, public shaming, or misconduct hunting, but on responsible post-publication peer review through:

1. Letters to the editor
2. Formal journal inquiries
3. Conceptual and interpretive checks
4. Conflict of interest and funding transparency checks
5. Human-reviewed use of AI for literature screening

## Core Principle

Published articles remain open to scholarly reading after publication.

Readers may identify:

- conceptual ambiguity
- mismatch between measurement and interpretation
- overstatement of conclusions
- insufficient disclosure of conflicts of interest
- missing funding or institutional relationship information
- unclear policy, advocacy, commercial, or professional roles

When concerns are substantial, they should be addressed through appropriate scholarly channels, such as letters to the editor, journal correspondence, or formal inquiries to the editorial office.

## What This Toolkit Does Not Do

This repository does not provide tools for accusing individual researchers of misconduct.

It does not encourage harassment, public shaming, social media campaigns, or informal retaliation.

AI may be used to support literature screening, summarization, and organization, but it must not replace human judgment. Any concern identified with AI assistance should be reviewed by a human reader before contacting a journal, publisher, or institution.

## Basic Position

Post-publication peer review is not an attack on authors.

It is a reader’s contribution to the accuracy, transparency, and interpretability of the scholarly record.

## Note on AI and Reviewer Education

This toolkit does not assume that AI can replace human reading, judgment, or reviewer training.

Post-publication peer review requires careful human reading. Readers should examine the article itself, including the research question, conceptual definitions, methods, measurements, interpretation, funding statements, and conflict-of-interest disclosures.

Using AI to perform the entire review process may undermine reviewer education. If students, trainees, or early-career researchers rely on AI-generated judgments without reading the article themselves, they may lose the opportunity to develop critical scholarly judgment.

However, in an era of information overload, AI tools may help readers identify candidate articles, organize bibliographic information, extract disclosure statements, summarize sections for preliminary review, and reduce the risk of overlooking relevant issues.

AI should therefore be used as a support tool for human review, not as a substitute for scholarly reading.
