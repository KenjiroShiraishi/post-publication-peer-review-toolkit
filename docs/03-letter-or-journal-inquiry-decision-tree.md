# Letter or Journal Inquiry Decision Tree

This document helps readers decide whether a concern identified after publication is better addressed through a letter to the editor, a formal journal inquiry, or no further action.

The goal is not to accuse authors, but to choose the most appropriate scholarly channel for responsible post-publication peer review.

## Core Principle

Different concerns require different channels.

A letter to the editor is usually appropriate when the issue concerns scholarly interpretation, conceptual clarity, measurement, methodology, or statistical inference.

A formal journal inquiry is usually appropriate when the issue concerns the accuracy, transparency, or completeness of the publication record, such as conflict of interest disclosure, funding information, authorship, affiliation, or correction needs.

## Step 1: Identify the Type of Concern

### A. Conceptual or Interpretive Concern

Use this category when the issue concerns:

- unclear concepts
- mismatch between concept and measurement
- overinterpretation of findings
- causal claims from non-causal designs
- clinical claims that exceed the data
- policy or practice recommendations stronger than the evidence supports
- subjective outcomes interpreted as proof of mechanism
- screening results treated as diagnoses
- proxy variables treated as the phenomenon itself

These issues are often suitable for a letter to the editor.

### B. Statistical or Methodological Concern

Use this category when the issue concerns:

- inappropriate statistical interpretation
- insufficient discussion of uncertainty
- confidence intervals ignored or underused
- subgroup analyses overinterpreted
- multiple comparisons not adequately addressed
- statistical significance treated as clinical importance
- noninferiority, equivalence, or superiority claims not aligned with the study design
- observational or cross-sectional data used to imply causality

These issues may be suitable for a letter to the editor, especially when the concern can be explained briefly and constructively.

### C. Disclosure or Transparency Concern

Use this category when the issue concerns:

- missing or incomplete conflict of interest disclosure
- unclear funding statement
- unclear role of funders
- missing institutional relationships
- relevant affiliations not disclosed
- relevant commercial, advocacy, political, or policy roles not disclosed
- possible need for a correction, corrigendum, erratum, or updated disclosure statement

These issues are often better addressed through a formal journal inquiry, because they may require editorial review, author clarification, or correction of the publication record.

## Step 2: Choose the Appropriate Channel

## Use a Letter to the Editor When

A letter may be appropriate when:

- the concern is about interpretation, not misconduct
- the concern can be explained from the published article
- the issue would help readers interpret the article more carefully
- the concern is narrow enough for a short scholarly response
- the article’s conclusion, framing, or implication may need clarification
- the journal accepts letters, comments, correspondence, or post-publication responses

Examples:

- A cross-sectional study implies causality.
- A screening tool is discussed as if it provides a diagnosis.
- A subjective outcome is interpreted as proof of biological mechanism.
- A small exploratory study makes broad policy recommendations.
- A noninferiority claim does not match the decision rule or margin.
- A study reports statistical significance but does not discuss clinical importance.

## Use a Formal Journal Inquiry When

A formal journal inquiry may be appropriate when:

- the concern involves missing or incomplete disclosure
- the issue may require correction of the article record
- the concern relates to conflict of interest, funding, affiliation, authorship, ethics approval, or data availability
- the issue cannot be adequately handled as scholarly debate
- the editorial office needs to verify information with the authors
- a correction, corrigendum, erratum, expression of concern, or other editorial action may be needed

Examples:

- The article states “no conflicts of interest,” but public information suggests relevant financial or institutional relationships.
- Funding is mentioned elsewhere but not clearly disclosed in the article.
- Authors have relevant organizational, advocacy, commercial, political, or policy roles that may affect reader interpretation.
- The role of the funder is unclear.
- The article appears to omit an important affiliation or institutional relationship.
- A correction may be needed to update the disclosure statement.

## Use No Further Action When

No further action may be appropriate when:

- the concern is minor and does not affect interpretation
- the issue is speculative
- the concern cannot be supported by public evidence
- the article already discloses the relevant information
- the concern is based mainly on disagreement rather than a clear scholarly issue
- the issue would be better addressed by reading more background literature
- the concern is too broad, vague, or personal to be useful

## Step 3: Apply the Reader-Relevance Test

Before writing a letter or inquiry, ask:

Would a reasonable reader need this clarification in order to interpret the article responsibly?

If the answer is yes, further action may be justified.

If the answer is no, the concern may be better kept as a private reading note.

## Step 4: Apply the Proportionality Test

The response should be proportional to the concern.

| Concern Type | Possible Response |
|---|---|
| Minor wording issue | No action or private note |
| Conceptual ambiguity | Letter to the editor |
| Interpretive overstatement | Letter to the editor |
| Statistical interpretation issue | Letter to the editor |
| Missing funding information | Journal inquiry |
| Incomplete conflict of interest disclosure | Journal inquiry |
| Possible need for correction | Journal inquiry |
| Serious integrity concern | Follow journal or publisher ethics process |

## Step 5: Avoid Inappropriate Use

Do not use letters or journal inquiries for:

- personal criticism
- public shaming
- retaliation
- social media campaigns
- unsupported allegations
- accusations generated by AI without human review
- disagreements that cannot be tied to the published article
- confidential or non-public information obtained without permission

Post-publication peer review should improve the scholarly record, not target individuals.

## Step 6: Human Review Before Action

Before submitting a letter or contacting a journal, a human reviewer should check:

- the full article
- the relevant disclosure statements
- the funding statement
- the article type
- the journal’s letter or correction policy
- the strength of the evidence
- whether the concern is narrow and clearly explainable
- whether the proposed action is proportionate

AI may assist with summarizing, organizing, or identifying possible issues, but it must not replace human judgment.

## Suggested Workflow

1. Read the article.
2. Identify the concern.
3. Classify the concern:
   - conceptual
   - interpretive
   - statistical
   - disclosure-related
   - other
4. Decide whether the concern affects reader interpretation.
5. Decide whether the concern is suitable for:
   - no action
   - private note
   - letter to the editor
   - formal journal inquiry
6. Prepare a neutral, evidence-based note.
7. Avoid personal accusations.
8. Use the journal’s official channel.
9. Keep a private record of what was submitted and when.
10. Wait for the editorial process.

## Suggested Language for a Letter to the Editor

A letter should usually be concise, specific, and scholarly.

Example opening:

> We read the article with interest and appreciate the authors’ contribution. We would like to raise a question regarding the interpretation of the findings.

Example framing:

> Our concern is not with the value of the study itself, but with whether the conclusion may exceed what the study design and measurements can support.

Example closing:

> Clarifying this point may help readers interpret the findings more cautiously and may support more precise future research.

## Suggested Language for a Journal Inquiry

A journal inquiry should be neutral and factual.

Example opening:

> I am writing as a reader to ask whether the journal may wish to review the conflict of interest and funding disclosure statement for the following article.

Example framing:

> I do not wish to make any allegation of misconduct. My concern is limited to whether the published disclosure provides sufficient information for readers to interpret the article.

Example closing:

> I leave it to the editorial office to determine whether any clarification or correction is warranted.

## Final Note

A letter to the editor is a form of scholarly dialogue.

A journal inquiry is a request for editorial review.

Neither should be used as a substitute for careful reading, fair interpretation, or appropriate human judgment.

Responsible post-publication peer review helps maintain the accuracy, transparency, and interpretability of the scholarly record.
