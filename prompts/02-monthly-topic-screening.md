# Prompt: Monthly Topic-Based Literature Screening

This prompt is designed to help readers identify recently published academic articles on a specific topic for responsible post-publication peer review.

The purpose is not to accuse authors or identify misconduct.

The purpose is to support human review by finding candidate articles that may deserve careful reading for conceptual clarity, interpretive balance, statistical inference, and disclosure transparency.

## Intended Use

Use this prompt when you want to screen recently published articles on a specific topic, such as:

- psychedelic-assisted therapy
- psilocybin treatment
- MDMA-assisted therapy
- ketamine-assisted psychotherapy
- acupuncture clinical trials
- complementary and alternative medicine
- cannabis and cannabinoid research
- public health policy
- AI in medicine
- patient safety
- clinical communication
- health ethics

This prompt is especially useful when a topic involves clinical claims, subjective outcomes, commercial interests, regulatory debates, public health implications, or vulnerable populations.

## Prompt

Find academic articles published within the last one month on the following topic:

**[INSERT TOPIC]**

Please search public academic sources where possible, including PubMed, Crossref, publisher websites, journal websites, and other publicly accessible scholarly databases.

For each article, create a table with the following columns:

- Title
- Authors
- Journal
- Publication date
- DOI or URL
- Article type
- Study type
- Intervention, exposure, or main subject
- Main outcome measures
- Main claim or conclusion
- Conflict of interest statement
- Funding statement
- Possible conceptual issue
- Possible meaning or interpretation issue
- Possible statistical inference issue
- Possible disclosure or transparency issue
- Priority for human review: High / Medium / Low / Unclear

## Screening Focus

For each article, check whether human review may be useful because of:

### 1. Conceptual Issues

- unclear definition of the central concept
- mismatch between concept and measurement
- screening results treated as diagnoses
- proxy variables treated as the phenomenon itself
- complex clinical, psychological, social, or ethical concepts reduced too quickly to simple scores

### 2. Meaning and Interpretation Issues

- conclusions that exceed the study design
- association interpreted as causation
- subjective improvement treated as proof of mechanism
- limited data used to support broad clinical or policy claims
- social, ethical, or cultural meanings added without sufficient support

### 3. Statistical Inference Issues

- small sample size relative to the claim
- confidence intervals or uncertainty under-discussed
- subgroup analyses overinterpreted
- multiple comparisons not adequately addressed
- statistical significance treated as clinical importance
- noninferiority, equivalence, or superiority claims not aligned with the study design
- causal language used for observational or cross-sectional data

### 4. Disclosure and Transparency Issues

- unclear conflict of interest disclosure
- unclear funding statement
- unclear role of funders
- relevant commercial, advocacy, political, institutional, or policy roles
- topic sensitivity involving clinical care, public health, regulation, commercial products, or vulnerable populations

## Important Limits

Do not accuse authors of misconduct.

Do not infer undisclosed conflicts of interest without evidence.

Do not rely on AI output alone.

AI should only identify candidate articles for human review.

A human reviewer must read the original article, disclosure statement, funding statement, author affiliations, methods, results, and discussion before any letter, journal inquiry, institutional communication, or public comment is prepared.

## Human Review Reminder

For each flagged article, the human reviewer should ask:

- What is the article actually claiming?
- What concept is being studied?
- How was the concept measured?
- Does the interpretation match the study design?
- Are statistical claims proportionate to the data?
- Are conflicts of interest, funding, affiliations, and institutional relationships disclosed clearly enough for readers?
- Would a reasonable reader need additional information to interpret the article responsibly?

## Suggested Output Format

After the table, provide a short narrative summary with the following sections:

### Summary of Search Results

Briefly summarize how many articles were found and what kinds of studies they were.

### Articles That May Deserve Human Review

List articles that may deserve closer reading, with a short reason for each.

### Possible Letter Candidates

Identify articles where the issue appears to concern scholarly interpretation, conceptual clarity, measurement, or statistical inference.

### Possible Journal Inquiry Candidates

Identify articles where the issue appears to concern disclosure, funding, affiliation, correction, or transparency of the publication record.

### No Immediate Action

List articles that appear low priority or do not currently require further action.

## Final Reminder

The items listed above are not findings of misconduct.

They are candidate articles for careful human review as part of responsible post-publication peer review.

AI may help organize the literature, but scholarly judgment must remain human.
